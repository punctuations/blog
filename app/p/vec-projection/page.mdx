You and I see in 2D, yet we live in a 3D space. As we percieve objects in a 3D space we only are able to see the 2D face that is, well, facing towards us. 
Cameras attempt to mimic our eyes and capture what we see into a more permanent form, but how can we simulate that? How can games, graphics, AR/VR, etc. see a 2D perspective in a 3D space?

There are some different ways to tackle this problem, that being, matrix projection approach and the plane/projection approach. Here I'll be covering the latter.

This problem is assuming that there is a camera, floating around at some random coordinate point $\vec{c} = (c_x,c_y,c_z)$. This camera is oriented around a [3D spherical plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system) *(for some context, when referring to angles, mathematical convention is used.)* where the camera is always facing toward the origin.

So, when the camera is *lets say,* at the point $(2 \hat{i}, 3 \hat{j}, 1 \hat{k})$, represented by the vector $\vec{c}$. There is also another vector, $\vec{v}$ on this place, coming from the origin to the point $(1 \hat{i}, 1 \hat{j}, 1 \hat{k})$. So, given that the camera is $\vec{c}$ what would the vector, $\vec{v}$ look like from the position of the camera?

The little orange rectangle depicted in the figure on the right is a representation of the plane of the camera, that is, a flat ground where an arrow straight to the point $\vec{c}$ would be perpendicular ($\perp$, i.e. $90\deg$ from the plane) to said ground. <sup>(note: this is also known as a normal vector, $\vec{n}$)</sup>

We now need to normalize the vector $\vec{c}$ so that it matches up with the height of $\vec{v}$ on the new plane (essentially making a right-angle triangle on the new plane), to do this we have to project $\vec{v}$ on this plane by calculating $proj_{\vec{c}}(\vec{v})$ (the projection of $\vec{v}$ w.r.t<sup><a href="#footer">[1]</a></sup>/onto $\vec{c}$). In order to calculate this we can simply follow the formula as such:

$$
proj_{\vec{c}}(\vec{v})=(\frac{\vec{v}\cdot\vec{c}}{\vec{c}\cdot\vec{c}})\vec{c}
$$

Please take into account that this diagram is *not* the best representation of the described behaviour, but it is mathematically the same. Considering that at this point we will now have the original vector, and a projection of $\vec{v}$ onto $\vec{c}$, we can calculate the real projection, $\vec{p}$, which will be the subtraction between the two vectors as such: 
$$
\vec{p}=\vec{v}-proj_{\vec{c}}(\vec{v})
$$
After the projection is found a scalar is applied to it determined by the distance the camera is away from the vector in focus. The scaling formula is the following: 
$$
\vec{p}_v = \begin{cases} 
               \frac{1}{d}\cdot\vec{p} & d\neq 0 \\
               0 & d=0
            \end{cases}
$$
as $\lim\limits_{d \to 0}=\infty$, since $\infty$ is computationally impossible $\vec{p}_v$ is set as 0 instead. 

(i.e. cannot divide by zero)
